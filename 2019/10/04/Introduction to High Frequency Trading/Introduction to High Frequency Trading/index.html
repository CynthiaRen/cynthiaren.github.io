Introduction to High Frequency Trading

Posted on 2019-10-04 Edited on 2019-10-17 In high frequency trading

High Frequecy Trading has great advantage over traditional tradings. This superiority brings along risk and also potential unfairness in the market. This post introduces what high frequency trading is, with some of the risk management techniques. In the U.S market, regulations prohibit several kinds of actions, including order triggering, spoofing and quote stuffing.

Introduction ↩

High frequency trading is a special kind of algorithmic trading associated with high speed and low latency. Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) characterized it as :

  • Using algorithms to complete decision making, order generating, transaction excecution, etc.
  • Short delay in order to minimize reaction time
  • High speed for orders to enter the system and connect to the market
  • Having large information set, in other words, continuous actions of sending and withdrawing orders

There have been debates on the pros and cons of this algorithm’s impact on the market. Some researches indicate that high frequency trading provides liquidity to the market and make the price more efficient. However, others propose two potential risks:

  1. The unstable liquidity high frequency trading provides may lead to a fragile market structure
  2. The advantage brought by high speed along with information asymmetry may lead to voliation of market fairness

For example, high frequency traders did aggravate the crush of the U.S market on 2010.05.06, if not to be blamed.

Risk Management ↩

Common risk management techniques in high frequency trading include:

  1. Watching out for the parameters: Human input is inevitable in almost all trading procedures. To avoid mistakes that may occur when filling in parameters, setting acceptable ranges for parameters and examination during initialization are essential.
  2. Control the number of unfilled orders: Inappropriately designed strategy may cause considerable orders to be triggered at the same time and then exposed to the market. You will experience not only unexpected positions when the market moves in an adverse way, but also the risk that other traders take advantage of this. One approach to this problem is simply setting a maximum number of exposed orders.
  3. Calculate live PnL and set thresholds: It’s a quite traditional stop-loss method applied in trading strategies. If a trading strategy works normally, it’s supposed to have positive expected return. Therefore, if the loss exceeds some pre-set threshold, the strategy will forxe exit positions and cut loss. There are also similar stop signals. For instance, the “Greek limit” in option market making system.
  4. Emergency mechanism: The emergency mechanism deals with technical issues that happen to the counter or exchange system. When connection with the exchange is lost, the trading program should try to cancel all orders and close itself. A backup counter system should also be prepared to cancel orders, especially for option trading.

Order Triggering ↩

Order Trigerring refers to causing feint in the market by sudden buldge trades of a certain object. While seducing others to follow, the trader did opposite trade to make profit. For high frequency traders, this kind of timings are easy to find using algos.

Spoofing ↩

Spoofing means placing and withdrawing orders not aiming at concluding transactions. Taking advantage of high speed, high frequency traders can place two orders with opposiote direction in the market. The larger order is used to disturb and deceive other investors while the smaller one shows the real intention. When the price moves towards the direction the trader wants, he or she cancel the larger order, execute the smaller one and make profit.

Quote Stuffing ↩

High Frequency traders can send bid and ask orders with huge amounts of contracts in less than a second. When the amount of orders is beyond the maximum capacity of the exchange server, the trading speed and the information trasferring speed goes down, so does the trasferring speed of price information. Then, high frequency traders can take advantage of the information asymmetry (since other investors are not receiving real time prices) and make profit.
